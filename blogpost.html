When God's Silence
Is His Answer

Sometimes the most profound thing God does is say nothing — and in that silence, He is shaping something in us that words never could.

PE
Pastor Emmanuel
Senior Pastor, Word Transformation Church
March 9, 2026
7 min read
14 comments
John 11:1–44
Morning light in the sanctuary — a place where silence speaks. Photo: WTC Media Team

Martha sent word to Jesus: "Lord, the one you love is sick." And Jesus, who loved Lazarus, who loved Martha and Mary — did nothing. He stayed where he was for two more days. No rushing. No immediate miracle. Just silence.

If you have ever prayed and felt like your prayer hit the ceiling, you know this feeling. If you have ever stood at the edge of a crisis — a diagnosis, a broken relationship, a financial collapse — and cried out to God and heard nothing back, this passage is for you.

"When he heard that Lazarus was sick, he stayed where he was two more days."

— John 11:6 (NIV)

Silence Is Not Absence

We tend to interpret God's silence as God's absence. If He is not speaking, we reason, He must not be listening. If He is not moving, He must not care. But the story of Lazarus dismantles this logic completely. Jesus knew exactly what was happening. He was not uninformed. He was not indifferent. He was intentional.

The text tells us in verse 5: "Now Jesus loved Martha and her sister and Lazarus." The Gospel writer places that declaration of love right before describing Jesus' decision to stay put. The love and the delay are not in contradiction. The love is the reason for the delay.

Reflection: Think of a time when God's silence felt like abandonment. Looking back now, can you see evidence of His hand working in that season — even if you could not see it at the time?

God Works on a Different Timeline

Jesus says something remarkable when he finally tells his disciples it is time to go: "Lazarus has fallen asleep; but I am going there to wake him up." From where Jesus stands — knowing the end from the beginning — what looks like death to everyone else is merely sleep. What looks like the worst possible outcome is actually the setup for the greatest possible testimony.

This is perhaps the most disorienting truth in all of Christian faith: God's delays are not God's denials. He operates on a timeline that accounts for resurrection, not just recovery. He is not trying to prevent you from going through hard things. He is shaping you through them into someone who can carry a greater weight of glory.

✦

What to Do in the Silence

So how do we live faithfully when God is quiet? Three things stand out from this passage:

  • Keep sending word. Martha and Mary sent word to Jesus. They did not stop praying because He had not yet come. Keep talking to Him even when He feels far.
  • Hold your theology steady. When Martha finally confronted Jesus, she said: "Lord, if you had been here, my brother would not have died. But I know that even now God will give you whatever you ask." Even in grief, her faith held. She did not abandon what she knew to be true.
  • Expect something you have not seen before. Nobody in that story expected Lazarus to walk out of the tomb. When God finally moves in your silence, it often exceeds anything you had imagined or prayed for.

"Did I not tell you that if you believe, you will see the glory of God?"

— John 11:40 (NIV)

A Word for Today

Perhaps you are in a season of silence right now. Perhaps you have been praying for a marriage, for a child, for a job, for healing — and heaven seems closed. I want you to hear this: God's silence is not His verdict on your situation. It is often the space He creates to do something that goes far beyond what you were asking for.

The story of Lazarus does not end with a recovered sick man. It ends with a dead man walking. God is not interested in merely improving your circumstances. He is interested in demonstrating His resurrection power in the very place where hope has died.

Keep trusting. Keep praying. The stone is about to be rolled away.
